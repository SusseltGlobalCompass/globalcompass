Le Baccalauréat Français

France's national school-leaving examination — one of Europe's oldest and most intellectually demanding qualifications, and the gateway to universities across the French-speaking world and beyond.

What it actually is

The French Baccalauréat (known simply as "le Bac") is France's national school-leaving qualification, completed at the end of Terminale — the final year of Lycée (roughly equivalent to Year 13 or Grade 12). It combines continuous assessment throughout the year with final written and oral examinations.

Since a major reform in 2021, students now choose two "spécialités" (specialist subjects) they study in depth in Terminale, alongside mandatory common subjects including Philosophy and the Grand Oral — a significant 20-minute oral presentation assessed by a panel, which has no direct equivalent in other international curricula.

Who follows it internationally

The French Bac is offered through the AEFE (Agence pour l'Enseignement Français à l'Étranger) network of 540+ French schools in 139 countries — making it one of the most geographically widespread national curricula in the world. Lycée Français schools exist across the Middle East, Africa, Asia, the Americas, and Europe.

For French expat families, the AEFE network offers unparalleled continuity — a student can move between Paris, Dubai, Singapore, and New York on the French curriculum with minimal disruption. For non-French families, the Bac's reputation and the Grand Oral's communication demands make it genuinely distinctive.

A brief history
1808
Napoléon creates the Baccalauréat
Napoléon Bonaparte establishes the Baccalauréat by imperial decree in 1808, making France one of the first countries to create a standardised national school-leaving qualification. The first examination is entirely oral — written papers come later.
1960s–90s
Global expansion through the AEFE network
France systematically builds a global network of Lycée Français schools through its cultural diplomacy strategy, eventually reaching 540+ schools in 139 countries. The Bac becomes the qualification of choice for French diplomatic and corporate expat families worldwide.
2021
The most significant reform in a generation
A major overhaul replaces the old series (S, ES, L) with a more flexible spécialités system, introduces continuous assessment for the first time, and adds the Grand Oral — a 20-minute oral examination on a topic of the student's choice, connecting their spécialités to a broader question.
The spécialités system

In Première, students choose three spécialités. In Terminale, they drop one and continue with two. The combination chosen sends a clear signal about academic direction.

The Grand Oral — what makes the Bac unique

No other major international curriculum has anything quite like the Grand Oral. In their final year, every Bac student prepares two questions connecting their two spécialités to a broader theme of their choice, then presents and defends one in a 20-minute oral examination before a panel of two examiners.

The final 5 minutes ask the student to reflect on how this question connects to their future studies and career ambitions. It is a genuine test of intellectual maturity and oral fluency — and it produces students who can articulate ideas under pressure in a way that surprises many university professors.

French Bac vs IB — honest comparison

Both are rigorous, internationally respected qualifications. The IB requires six subjects plus TOK, CAS, and an Extended Essay — genuinely broad. The French Bac has mandatory common subjects (including Philosophy, which every student studies regardless of direction) but allows deeper specialisation through the spécialités.

The Grand Oral has no IB equivalent and is genuinely distinctive on a university application. French universities treat the Bac as their primary entry qualification. For non-French universities, both IB and Bac are well recognised — though the Bac's grading scale (out of 20, with 14–15 being genuinely excellent) can sometimes confuse admissions officers unfamiliar with it.

Who tends to thrive

Students who thrive in the French Bac tend to be strong writers and analytical thinkers — Philosophy is compulsory for everyone, and the French academic tradition places enormous value on structured argumentation (the dissertation) and the ability to construct a coherent, well-reasoned essay under exam conditions.

The Grand Oral also rewards students who are genuinely curious and can connect ideas across disciplines. Students who struggle most are those who want purely practical, skills-based learning — the Bac is unapologetically academic and theory-heavy, which is either a strength or a frustration depending on the student.
