Indian CBSE

The Central Board of Secondary Education — one of the world's largest school systems, followed by 25 million students across India and in Indian schools internationally.

What it actually is

CBSE is India's national school curriculum, governed directly by the Government of India. It runs from Class 1 through Class 12, with the two most important public examinations being the Class 10 boards (AISSE) and the Class 12 boards (AISSCE) — the results that determine university admission in India.

The curriculum is known for its rigour in mathematics and science, and for producing some of the world's highest-performing students in engineering and medical entrance examinations. Over 25,000 schools in India and more than 240 schools in 28 countries follow the CBSE framework.

Who follows it internationally

Indian schools across the UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Oman, Bahrain, Singapore, and Malaysia largely follow the CBSE curriculum. For Indian expat families, CBSE schools abroad offer the advantage of continuity — a student who moves between countries on this curriculum faces minimal disruption to their academic progression.

For non-Indian families, CBSE is worth understanding because its graduates compete fiercely for university places globally. A strong CBSE Class 12 result is increasingly recognised by universities in the UK, Canada, and Australia as a credible entry qualification.

How CBSE developed
1929
Board of High School established
The precursor to CBSE is established in Ajmer to oversee secondary education examinations across parts of British India. The board evolves through independence and several reorganisations into the Central Board of Secondary Education we know today.
1962
CBSE formally constituted
The Central Board of Secondary Education is formally established by the Government of India, absorbing several regional boards and creating a single national framework for schools affiliated with central government institutions, including Kendriya Vidyalayas and Jawahar Navodaya Vidyalayas.
Today
The world's largest school board
With over 25,000 affiliated schools and 25 million students, CBSE is one of the largest educational boards on the planet. Its Class 12 results feed directly into India's Joint Entrance Examination (JEE) for engineering and NEET for medicine — two of the world's most competitive university entrance examinations.
The three streams in Class 11-12

At Class 11, CBSE students choose one of three academic streams based on their intended career direction. This is one of the most consequential decisions in the Indian education system.

Science Stream
Physics, Chemistry, Biology or Mathematics, English. Gateway to Medicine (NEET) and Engineering (JEE).
Commerce Stream
Accountancy, Business Studies, Economics, Mathematics or Informatics. Gateway to CA, MBA, and finance careers.
Humanities Stream
History, Geography, Political Science, Psychology, Sociology. Gateway to law, journalism, civil services, and social sciences.
Grading and CGPA explained

CBSE uses a 9-point grading scale for internal assessments (Class 10) and percentage marks for Class 12 board examinations. The CGPA (Cumulative Grade Point Average) used at Class 10 is calculated from a student's best five subjects.

For Class 12 — the university-entry examination — results are expressed as a percentage. An aggregate above 90% is considered highly competitive for top Indian universities. For international university applications, CBSE percentages are typically converted using each university's own equivalency tables.

CBSE vs IB — honest comparison

CBSE is a content-heavy, examination-focused curriculum with a clear national purpose: preparing students for India's university entrance system. The IB is internationally designed, broader, and places more weight on independent thinking, research, and community engagement.

For families weighing both: CBSE gives a student the strongest possible preparation for Indian university entrance examinations. The IB gives flexibility across global university systems and a more holistic academic experience. Indian families planning to return to India for university tend to favour CBSE; those planning international university entry often consider both options carefully.

Who tends to thrive

Students who thrive in CBSE are typically disciplined, exam-focused, and motivated by clear academic benchmarks. The system rewards consistent hard work and mastery of structured content — qualities that also serve students well in India's intensely competitive entrance examinations.

The curriculum's rigour in mathematics and science means CBSE students who go on to international universities in STEM fields often find the first year of university relatively manageable — the foundational content is strong. Where CBSE students sometimes need adjustment is in research writing, independent project work, and critical analysis — skills that IB and A-Level students develop more explicitly.
