The International
Baccalaureate (IB)

A single educational framework taught the same way in over 160 countries - built specifically for families who move. Here is what it actually is, where it came from, and who it tends to suit.

What Is The IB?

The IB isn't tied to any single country's school system. Instead of memorizing facts for one national exam, IB students are pushed to think critically, write independently, and connect ideas across subjects rather than studying them in isolation. It spans four age stages, from age 3 all the way to 19, and the qualification carries the same meaning whether a student finishes it in Dubai, Geneva, or Singapore.

The Story - How It Began

The idea is older than the organization itself. In 1948, a French educator named Marie-Therese Maurette asked an ambitious question: could education itself be designed to reduce conflict between nations? Two decades later, teachers at the International School of Geneva turned that idea into something practical - a single, portable qualification for the children of diplomats and international staff who kept relocating between mismatched school systems.

1948
Marie-Therese Maurette writes the founding idea: education designed for international understanding.
1968
The IB officially launches in Geneva, Switzerland, with 12 pilot schools across 12 countries.
1975
After a six-year experimental period, the first official IB Diploma is awarded.
1994 - 1997
The Middle Years Programme (1994) and Primary Years Programme (1997) launch.
2012
The Career-related Programme launches, blending academics with career skills.
Today
Over 6,200 schools teach the IB across 160+ countries, to nearly 2 million students.
The Four Programmes Today
PYP
Ages 3-12
Big, open questions rather than rigid subjects - encouraging young children to explore and ask "why."
MYP
Ages 11-16
Eight subject areas studied together, building toward a personal project.
DP
Ages 16-19
Six subjects at once, plus three distinctive extra requirements.
CP
Ages 16-19
Academic subjects blended with real career and practical skills training.
What Makes It Genuinely Different
Theory of Knowledge (TOK)
Asks "how do we actually know what we know?" The one requirement every Diploma student shares.
CAS - Creativity, Activity, Service
Real time outside the classroom: creating, staying active, contributing to community. Not graded.
The Extended Essay
An independent, 4,000-word research project on a topic the student chooses themselves.
Beyond The Classroom - Building Global Citizens
"
As an IB educator, I've seen firsthand how the curriculum's CAS requirement pushes students toward exactly this kind of global engagement - many of the most memorable Model United Nations delegates I've taught came directly out of the IB's emphasis on service and active citizenship.
- A GlobalCompass educator, former IB teacher

It's not a coincidence that IB students show up so often in Model United Nations halls around the world. The same CAS requirement that asks every student to genuinely serve their community also tends to push them naturally toward MUN - debating real global issues, representing countries they've never lived in, and practicing the exact diplomatic instincts the IB was originally built to cultivate back in 1968.

Who Tends To Thrive

In fairness to families comparing options: the IB suits curious, independent students who don't mind juggling six subjects plus three extra projects at once, and who enjoy writing and research as much as exams. It's a real workload - broader than something like A-Levels, which lets a student go deep into just 3-4 subjects instead. Neither approach is "better" - it depends on whether your child thrives with breadth or with depth.
