Australian VCE

The Victorian Certificate of Education — Australia's most widely recognised senior secondary qualification, and the gateway to Australian universities through one of the world's most transparent and data-driven admissions systems.

What it actually is

The VCE (Victorian Certificate of Education) is completed over two years (Years 11 and 12) in Victoria, Australia. Students typically study five or six subjects, with each subject assessed through a combination of School-Assessed Coursework (SAC) throughout the year and final external examinations in November.

Each subject produces a Study Score out of 50, which feeds into the ATAR — the Australian Tertiary Admission Rank — a percentile score from 0 to 99.95 that ranks students against their entire year cohort. The ATAR is the primary metric Australian universities use for admissions. An ATAR of 99 means a student has outperformed 99% of their peers.

Who follows it internationally

Australian curriculum international schools exist across Asia, the Middle East, and beyond — particularly in Singapore, Malaysia, the UAE, Qatar, and across Southeast Asia. Australian expat families typically seek out these schools for continuity, but the VCE is also a genuine option for non-Australian families planning to send their children to Australian universities.

Australia's university system is internationally respected and significantly more affordable than the UK or US for international students — tuition typically runs AUD 30,000–45,000 per year, compared to £35,000+ for UK universities. The Group of Eight (Go8) universities — including Melbourne, Sydney, ANU, and UNSW — compete directly with UK Russell Group institutions in global rankings.

The ATAR explained
How the ATAR is calculated

The ATAR is calculated from a student's best four subjects (counting English or an approved English equivalent as one of them), plus 10% of a fifth and sixth subject. Study Scores are scaled based on the difficulty of each subject — a score of 40 in Mathematical Methods carries more weight than 40 in an easier subject, because the ATAR system is designed to reward students who take harder courses.

This scaling system matters for subject choices: students aiming for competitive ATARs are often advised to include at least one mathematics and one science subject, both of which tend to scale positively. The exact scaling varies each year based on how cohorts perform.

What ATAR scores mean in practice

Medicine at the University of Melbourne typically requires an ATAR of 99.00 or above — meaning a student must outperform 99% of all VCE students in Victoria. Law at top universities requires 97+. Most engineering programmes sit between 85–95. A wide range of good university programmes are accessible with an ATAR between 70 and 85.

For international families: the ATAR is one of the most transparent university admissions systems in the world. Unlike the holistic US system or the contextualised UK system, the ATAR is a single, publicly understood number — families always know exactly where they stand.

How the VCE developed
1987
VCE replaces the Higher School Certificate
Victoria introduces the VCE to replace the existing Higher School Certificate, creating a more flexible and modular senior secondary qualification. The new system allows students to choose from a broader range of subjects while maintaining rigorous assessment standards.
2009
ATAR introduced nationally
Australia replaces its various state-based university admission indices with the unified Australian Tertiary Admission Rank (ATAR), creating a nationally consistent measure. This makes it easier for students to apply to universities across different Australian states and allows direct comparison across different senior certificates.
Today
Growing international recognition
VCE results and ATARs are increasingly recognised by universities in the UK, Canada, Singapore, and Hong Kong. Australian universities themselves attract significant international student populations, making VCE schools internationally an increasingly popular choice for families considering Australian higher education.

VCE vs IB — honest comparison

The IB is more internationally portable and explicitly designed for students unsure of their destination country. The VCE is optimised specifically for Australian university entry — if a family is confident their child will study in Australia, the VCE is the most straightforward pathway.

The IB requires six subjects plus TOK, CAS, and an Extended Essay — a broader, more demanding total programme. VCE students study five to six subjects without the IB's mandatory extras, which can make the workload feel more manageable, though the November examination pressure is intense and concentrated.

Who tends to thrive

Students who thrive in the VCE tend to be exam-focused and good at managing performance under pressure — the November exam period is intense and the ATAR calculation means every percentage point matters. The School-Assessed Coursework (SAC) component also rewards students who work consistently throughout the year rather than relying on last-minute exam preparation.

For expat families considering the VCE: it is worth being clear-eyed that the ATAR's value is primarily within Australia. Students who end up applying to UK, US, or other international universities will need to ensure their VCE results translate clearly — most top universities have established equivalency guides, but it requires more active management than an IB application.
